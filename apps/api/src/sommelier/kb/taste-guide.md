# Taste, richness & texture guide

This guide describes how the dishes on our menu actually eat — their flavor,
their richness, and their texture — so a recommendation can be matched to what a
guest is in the mood for. Every dish below is named exactly as it appears on the
menu; nothing here invents a dish, a price, or an ingredient we do not serve.

## What "rich", "fatty" and "buttery" mean here

Fattier cuts of tuna eat rich, buttery, and melt-in-the-mouth; the fat coats the
palate and the slice barely needs chewing. The richest experiences on this menu:

- **Otoro Selection** — the single richest item we serve. Otoro is the fattiest
  part of the bluefin belly, aged five days; it is intensely buttery and
  dissolves on the tongue. This is the dish to suggest for "something rich",
  "something fatty", "the most indulgent thing", or "melt-in-the-mouth".
- **Toro Truffle Roll** — fatty tuna (toro) layered with shaved black truffle.
  Rich and aromatic at once: the tuna brings the buttery fat, the truffle brings
  an earthy, savory perfume. Good for "rich but interesting" or "something
  luxurious with truffle".
- **Chef’s Omakase** — a twelve-piece chef selection that includes richer cuts
  among its range; a good answer for a guest who wants indulgence but also
  variety rather than a single rich note.

## What "light", "mild", "clean" and "fresh" mean here

Leaner cuts taste cleaner, more delicate, and more savory than fatty ones — less
coating fat, more of the fish’s natural sweetness and sea-fresh character.

- **Sashimi Moriawase** — seven different clean, delicate slices (hamachi,
  kanpachi, and more). The best answer for "something light", "something fresh",
  "mild", "not too heavy", or a guest who wants to taste a range of cleaner cuts
  side by side.
- **Ikura Don** — salmon roe over warm vinegared rice. Not a fatty dish: the
  texture is briny, juicy, and popping rather than buttery, and the warm rice
  keeps it comforting rather than heavy.

## Texture vocabulary

- **Melt-in-the-mouth / buttery** → Otoro Selection, Toro Truffle Roll.
- **Clean, delicate slices** → Sashimi Moriawase.
- **Briny, popping roe** → Ikura Don. Roe bursts rather than melts; a different
  pleasure from a fatty slice.
- **A bit of everything** → Chef’s Omakase and Couple’s Set both span several
  textures in one order (nigiri, maki, sashimi), so no single texture dominates.

## "Tuna" specifically

Several dishes are tuna-forward, which matters when a guest just says "tuna":

- **Otoro Selection** — premium fatty bluefin tuna belly; the most tuna-rich,
  most indulgent tuna choice.
- **Toro Truffle Roll** — fatty tuna (toro) in roll form, dressed with truffle.
- **Chef’s Omakase** and **Couple’s Set** — both typically include tuna among a
  broader selection, for a guest who wants tuna but not only tuna.

## Reading the menu’s categories

The menu is organized into categories that hint at format and portion: nigiri
(slices over pressed rice), maki (rolls), sashimi (slices, no rice), donburi (a
rice bowl), omakase (a chef-selected course), and sets (a larger shared
selection). When a guest asks for "a roll", a maki dish such as the Toro Truffle
Roll fits; for "just the fish, no rice", a sashimi dish such as the Sashimi
Moriawase fits; for "a rice bowl", the Ikura Don fits.
